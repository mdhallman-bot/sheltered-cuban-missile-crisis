📜 Historical Background

After World War II ended in 1945, two superpowers emerged: the United States, which promoted democracy and capitalism, and the Soviet Union, which spread communism across Eastern Europe and Asia. This rivalry — known as the Cold War — defined global politics for nearly five decades. Neither side ever directly fought the other, but they competed through proxy wars, nuclear arms buildups, and constant political pressure. By 1962, both nations had enough nuclear weapons to destroy each other many times over.

In April 1961, the United States supported a failed attempt by Cuban exiles to overthrow Fidel Castro, Cuba's communist leader. The botched operation — known as the Bay of Pigs — humiliated President Kennedy and pushed Castro closer to the Soviet Union. Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev, sensing an opportunity, secretly began shipping nuclear missiles to Cuba. When American spy planes discovered the missiles in October 1962, the world entered the most dangerous thirteen days in modern history.

Story 1
The Cuban Missile Crisis: Thirteen Days at the Edge
Read carefully — highlight important ideas, then explain your choices

On October 16, 1962, President John F. Kennedy was shown aerial photographs taken by a U-2 spy plane flying over Cuba. The images confirmed what intelligence analysts had feared: the Soviet Union was constructing nuclear missile launch sites capable of striking cities across the eastern United States within minutes. Kennedy immediately assembled ExComm, a secret committee of his most trusted advisers, and spent six days weighing his options before making any public announcement. His decisions over the next thirteen days would determine whether the world entered a nuclear war.

Kennedy faced three main choices. His military commanders, led by Air Force General Curtis LeMay, pushed aggressively for an immediate air strike to destroy the missile sites before they became operational. A second option was a full-scale naval invasion of Cuba. Kennedy rejected both — a military strike risked killing Soviet personnel and triggering a retaliatory attack on American allies in Europe. Instead, he chose a naval quarantine: US warships would surround Cuba and turn back any Soviet vessels carrying weapons. On October 22nd, Kennedy appeared on national television and announced the crisis to the American public for the first time, demanding the Soviet Union remove the missiles immediately.

Khrushchev initially refused to back down, sending Kennedy an angry letter accusing the United States of piracy on the high seas. Soviet ships continued steaming toward the blockade line. On October 24th, the moment of maximum tension arrived — Soviet vessels approached within miles of the US naval perimeter. Then, remarkably, they stopped. They turned around. Secretary of State Dean Rusk famously whispered, "We're eyeball to eyeball, and I think the other fellow just blinked." But the crisis was far from over. On October 27th — a day historians call "Black Saturday" — a US U-2 pilot, Major Rudolph Anderson, was shot down and killed over Cuba. Pressure mounted inside the US military for immediate retaliation. Kennedy, under enormous pressure, held back.

The resolution came through a combination of public negotiation and secret diplomacy. Khrushchev sent Kennedy two letters: the first offered a compromise; the second, harsher letter demanded the US remove its own nuclear missiles from Turkey. Kennedy, advised by his brother Robert Kennedy, chose to respond only to the first letter and ignore the second — while secretly agreeing to remove the Turkish missiles later. On October 28th, Khrushchev announced the Soviet missiles would be withdrawn from Cuba. The world exhaled. The crisis had lasted thirteen days and brought humanity closer to nuclear war than it had ever been before — or has been since.

Story 2
The Man Who Saved the World: Vasili Arkhipov
Read carefully — highlight important ideas, then explain your choices

While Kennedy and Khrushchev negotiated at the highest levels of global power, a far more immediate crisis was unfolding beneath the surface of the Atlantic Ocean. A Soviet submarine designated B-59 had been operating near Cuba for weeks, carrying conventional torpedoes — and one torpedo armed with a nuclear warhead. The submarine had lost contact with Moscow days earlier and had received no radio transmissions since the crisis escalated. The crew had no reliable information about what was happening above the surface. For all they knew, the war had already begun.

Conditions inside the B-59 had become desperate. The submarine could not surface to recharge its batteries without risking detection and attack by US Navy ships searching the area. The internal temperature had climbed above 100 degrees Fahrenheit. Carbon dioxide levels were rising dangerously, causing crew members to lose consciousness. The captain, Valentin Savitsky, had been awake for days. When American destroyers began dropping small depth charges near the submarine — a standard signal to surface, not intended as an attack — Savitsky interpreted them as acts of war. Convinced that World War III had already started, he ordered the nuclear torpedo to be prepared for launch.

Soviet military protocol required the authorization of three senior officers before a nuclear weapon could be fired: the submarine's captain, the political officer on board, and a senior officer who happened to be traveling on the B-59 on that particular mission. Captain Savitsky gave his authorization. The political officer, Ivan Maslennikov, agreed. But the third officer — Vasili Arkhipov, a flotilla commander who had previously survived a near-fatal nuclear accident aboard another submarine — refused. He was calm. He was methodical. He argued that without confirmed orders from Moscow, firing a nuclear weapon would be an unforgivable act that could trigger an all-out nuclear exchange. He insisted the submarine surface and attempt to make contact before taking any irreversible action.

Savitsky, exhausted and furious, argued with Arkhipov for what felt like an eternity. Eventually, he relented. The B-59 surfaced. US destroyers surrounded it immediately, but both sides stood down. No shots were fired. The torpedo was never launched. The world would not learn Arkhipov's name — or how close it had come to nuclear annihilation — for more than four decades. When his story was finally confirmed at a 2002 conference in Havana, American scholar Thomas Blanton stated simply: "A man named Arkhipov saved the world." The question his story forces us to ask is deeply uncomfortable: how many other moments like this one passed without anyone ever knowing?
